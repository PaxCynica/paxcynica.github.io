A History of Military Involvement

Indonesia's modern history is deeply intertwined with the military. Following independence in 1949, the nation struggled with political instability and economic challenges. This created an environment where the military gradually expanded its influence, culminating in the rise of General Suharto in the mid-1960s.

Suharto's "New Order" regime, which lasted for over three decades, was characterized by a strong military presence in all aspects of Indonesian society. The military played a dominant role in politics, the economy, and even social life. This era was marked by economic development but also by suppression of dissent, human rights abuses, and widespread corruption.

Key figures during this period included not only Suharto himself but also prominent military leaders who held significant positions in government and state-owned enterprises. The concept of "dwifungsi" (dual function) formalized the military's role in both defense and socio-political affairs, justifying its extensive involvement in civilian life.

The Transition to Democracy and its Fragility

The Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-98 triggered widespread protests and ultimately led to Suharto's downfall in 1998. This marked the beginning of Indonesia's transition to democracy, with reforms aimed at reducing the military's political power and promoting civilian control.

However, the transition has been uneven and incomplete. While Indonesia has made significant progress in establishing democratic institutions and processes, the military has retained considerable influence, particularly in certain regions and sectors. Long-term trends show a cyclical pattern, with periods of greater civilian control followed by resurgences of military influence, often driven by perceived threats to national security or stability.

Prabowo Subianto and the Resurgence

The election of Prabowo Subianto as president in 2024 is a significant development in this context. Prabowo, a former general with a controversial past linked to human rights abuses during the Suharto era, represents a potential shift in the balance of power. His appointment of military figures to key government positions and his rhetoric emphasizing national unity and security have fueled concerns about a return to authoritarian tendencies. Key figures now include not only Prabowo himself but also those military officials he has brought into his administration.

Potential Implications and Future Trajectories

The increasing military influence under Prabowo's leadership could have several implications:

  • Erosion of Democratic Institutions: The military's growing role in government could weaken civilian institutions and processes, undermining democratic checks and balances.
  • Restrictions on Freedom of the Press: Increased threats against reporters, as indicated in recent reports, could stifle independent journalism and limit public access to information.
  • Economic Instability: Concerns about a return to authoritarianism could deter foreign investment and hinder economic growth.
  • Human Rights Abuses: The military's involvement in internal security operations could lead to human rights violations, particularly in regions with ongoing conflicts or separatist movements.

Looking ahead, several scenarios are possible. Indonesia could continue on its democratic path, with civilian institutions effectively checking military power. Alternatively, the country could experience a gradual slide back towards authoritarianism, with the military playing an increasingly dominant role in all aspects of society. A third scenario involves a more abrupt shift, potentially triggered by a political crisis or a perceived threat to national security.

Implications & Significance

Understanding the historical context of military involvement in Indonesian politics is crucial for assessing the current situation. By recognizing the cyclical nature of civilian-military relations and the potential triggers for authoritarian resurgence, policymakers and observers can better anticipate future developments and work to safeguard Indonesia's democratic gains. The rise of military influence should be monitored closely, with a focus on protecting freedom of the press, strengthening civilian institutions, and promoting human rights.
